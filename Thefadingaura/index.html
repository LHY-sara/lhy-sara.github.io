The Fading Aura

Reclaiming the Spirit of Dadaocheng

PHOTO ESSAY

28th May, 2023

By LUO, Hui-Yun

The Spirit Returns

In Taipei's oldest district, a troupe of celestial bodyguards returns after 28 years to restore the soul of a community.

"The Eight Generals were the soundtrack of my junior high years. Hearing the jingle of their bells in the alleyways... it used to terrify me," laughs Huang Fei-lin.

Huang is the founder of "Shuxi Lane," a cultural preservation initiative, but today he is talking about something older than memory. To the locals of Dadaocheng—Taipei's historic river port—the Bajiajiang (Eight Generals) are not merely folklore. They are the fierce, painted-faced enforcers of the underworld, and their rise and fall mirrors the destiny of the district itself.

The Roots of the River Port

To understand the Generals, one must understand the soil they walk upon. The story of Dadaocheng began with refugees. In the mid-19th century, settlers from the Tong An clan in mainland China fled clan warfare in nearby Monga.

By the 1860s, the marshes had turned gold. Dadaocheng became a global trading hub for camphor, indigo, and tea. As wealth accumulated, merchant guilds began sponsoring cultural endeavors. In 1871, the "Ling An Club" was formed.

"This is an era where the 'aura' is fading. We are trying to retrieve our lost heart."

— Huang Fei-lin, Founder of Shuxi Lane

A Hollowed Culture

"You couldn't see the temple fairs anymore. Dadaocheng stopped talking about the City God," Huang recalls.

He describes a neighborhood in a state of "historical suspension." In recent years, attempts to revitalize the area have often leaned into commercialized kitsch. The City God—traditionally a stern magistrate of the afterlife who judges souls—was turned into a "DJ" for a cultural festival.

"From a theological perspective, the City God is a judicial deity of the underworld," Huang argues. Faith, he insists, is not just about bowing to statues. It is the connective tissue between traditional culture and daily life.

The Return of the Generals

This desire for authenticity sparked a revival. Leading the charge is Chen Zhong-xin, leader of the Taipei Eight Generals troupe. A child of the temple courtyard and a master tailor of traditional Qipao dresses, Chen holds a deep gratitude toward the City God.

"We are searching for a golden age of faith," Chen says. "We want to condense the community again to revive the culture."

Although the troupe ceased public patrols in 1995, the ember never truly died. Huang and Chen established the "Shuxi Lane" office, bridging the past and present through face-painting workshops and storytelling sessions.

From Sacred Bells to Silent Streets

"Looking at the troupe is like looking at a microcosm of the old city."

— Huang Fei-lin

A Society in Miniature

Huang emphasizes that the Eight Generals require the village to raise them. They do not perform for profit; they march only for the City God's birthday. The members are a cross-section of Taipei society: hoteliers, tea merchants, factory workers, market vendors, and IT professionals.

Unlike other troupes in Taipei that have evolved into different styles, the Dadaocheng faction insists on maintaining the most rustic, original form. Their return ceremony was an act of inheritance, picking up the baton dropped nearly three decades ago.

More Than a Ritual

For the participants, this is not performance art; it is a spiritual transaction.

"Many people come to serve with a heart full of huan-yuan—the desire to repay a vow to the gods," Huang explains.

One story stands out: A volunteer told Huang that for the upcoming City God birthday procession, he intends to bring his brother, who is battling cancer, to help serve the gods. For him, this faith isn't an empty ceremony. It contains deep care for his family and a pious wish for divine reciprocity.

"The point isn't the procession itself. It's the warmth within it."

— Huang Fei-lin

The Lingering Light

Having worked in cultural restoration for eight years, Huang has seen elders weep at the sight of the Generals, moved by the recovery of their own memories.

He views these collective prayers as a spiritual anchor. Through inheritance, generation by generation, he hopes to weave historical context back into the fabric of the city, restoring a sense of empathy and connection.

In Dadaocheng, many elders still believe the old district is a dimensional space where humans, gods, and spirits coexist. The loss of traditional culture implies the fading of this belief system.

"We set out again so that we can come together again," Huang says, his eyes shining. He looks forward to a future where the culture of the Eight Generals doesn't just survive but breathes.
